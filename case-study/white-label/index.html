Deep Problem Framing
The Goal

Every client wanted a branded investor portal. Canaccord can't look like Raymond James. But maintaining 154 separate codebases was impossible. We needed one design system that could serve any brand — adapting colors, logos, and typography per client while maintaining consistent UX across all of them.

The Problem

The early approach was per-client custom builds. Each new partnership meant forking the codebase, applying brand changes manually, and maintaining a separate deployment. At 5 clients this was manageable. At 20 it was painful. At 154 it would be impossible. Every bug fix had to be deployed 154 times. Every feature update required 154 separate QA passes. Brand consistency was unpredictable — some clients got the latest version, others were months behind.

How Might We

How might we build one design system that serves 154 client implementations — adapting colors, logos, typography per client while maintaining consistent UX?

Before Per-client builds Custom code per client. Unsustainable at scale. Bug fixes deployed 154 times.
After Token-based system One codebase, 154 implementations via design tokens. 5 inputs generate a complete portal.
Scale 85+ partnerships Serving clients across 6 countries (Canada, US, UK, Singapore, Italy, Ireland) from a single design system.
User Research
Methodology

Brand audits across 20+ client portals to identify common theming patterns. Interviews with client brand managers about their requirements. Analysis of onboarding timelines to measure how long brand setup took with the old per-client approach.

Sample

20+ client brand audits, 12 brand manager interviews, 8 years of iterative improvement across platform versions. Continuous research as new clients onboarded.

Key Insights
Brand is trust in financial services

Investors expect the portal to look and feel like their dealer's brand. A generic-looking portal created friction — investors questioned whether the platform was legitimate. Brand consistency directly impacted onboarding completion rates.

Clients don't want flexibility — they want simplicity

Early versions offered extensive customization. Clients were overwhelmed. What they actually wanted: upload a logo, pick a color, see their brand applied everywhere. 5 inputs, not 50 controls.

UX must be consistent across brands

While visual branding changed per client, the user experience — navigation, workflows, information hierarchy — had to remain identical. Support and training materials needed to work for all 154 client portals.

Design Approach / Solution

Our team designed a token-based theming system where 5 client inputs — logo, primary color, accent color, font, and favicon — generate a complete branded investor portal. The system cascades brand tokens through every component, from navigation to buttons to data tables.

The design system separated brand tokens (visual identity) from UX tokens (spacing, layout, interaction patterns). Brands could change freely without affecting usability. This meant we could onboard a new client in hours instead of weeks, and every client automatically received platform updates.

The 5-Input System

1. Logo upload → header, emails, documents, favicon

2. Primary color → navigation, headings, CTAs

3. Accent color → highlights, links, status indicators

4. Font selection → all typography across the portal

5. Favicon → browser tab, bookmarks, PWA icon

Reflection
✓ What Worked

1. The 5-input system eliminated client confusion — no one had to make 50 design decisions. Upload logo, pick colors, done.

2. Separating brand tokens from UX tokens meant we could update workflows for all 154 client implementations simultaneously without breaking anyone's brand

3. New client onboarding went from weeks of custom development to hours of token configuration

◈ What I'd Do Differently

1. Start with tokens from day one — the migration from per-client builds to tokens was painful and took over a year

2. Build a live preview tool earlier — clients wanted to see their brand applied before committing, but we built this feature late

3. Accessibility per brand — some client color choices created contrast issues that we caught too late in the process
