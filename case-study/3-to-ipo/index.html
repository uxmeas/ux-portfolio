The Journey
The Arc

This is not a product case study — it's a career story. Over 17 years, our design practice grew from a sole designer at a 3-person startup to a full team at a publicly traded fintech company. Along the way, I shaped every product the company shipped, built the design team, established the design system, and helped take the company from a garage to the TSX Venture Exchange.

The Challenge

At every stage, the design challenge changed. Early on, it was about shipping fast with no system. Then it was about establishing credibility with enterprise clients. Post-IPO, it was about scaling design practices across a growing team while maintaining the product quality that got us there. The constant: design had to drive business outcomes, not just make things look good.

Phase 1: Founder-Designer (2008–2012)

Three people in a room. No design system, no process, no team. I was the only designer — and also a co-founder, which meant design decisions were business decisions and vice versa.

This phase was about survival: shipping fast, learning from users in real time, and building the first version of a crowdfunding platform that would later pivot into capital markets. Every pixel I pushed was a bet on the company's future.

What Defined This Phase

1. Solo designer — every screen, every flow, every decision was mine

2. No system — shipped fast with raw CSS, no tokens, no components

3. Direct user feedback — watched real users struggle and iterated daily

4. Crowdfunding platform — donations, rewards, community fundraising

Phase 2: Product Designer → Lead (2012–2017)

The pivot that changed everything. We moved from crowdfunding into private capital markets — equity raises for investment dealers. Suddenly our users were enterprise compliance officers and fund managers, not indie artists and community donors.

This phase included InvestYYC (a Calgary investor marketplace), the EY Entrepreneur of the Year nomination, and the groundwork for IPO. Design had to grow up — from startup scrappy to enterprise credible.

What Defined This Phase

1. Pivoted from crowdfunding to capital markets — completely new user base

2. Enterprise clients demanded polish and compliance — no more MVP

3. EY Entrepreneur of the Year Finalist 2017 — company-level recognition

Phase 3: Lead → Head of Design (2017–2020)

Post-IPO, the company was publicly traded on the TSX Venture Exchange. With public scrutiny came new standards — every design decision now had potential regulatory and investor implications.

I formalized the design system, established design processes for a growing team, and began working with enterprise clients like Canaccord Genuity and Raymond James. This was the transition from individual contributor to design leader.

What Defined This Phase

1. Built formal design system — tokens, components, documentation

2. Enterprise clients — Canaccord, Raymond James, Sprott Capital, Markette Ventures (largest contract in company history)

3. Post-IPO design standards — public company scrutiny on product quality

4. Team growth — from solo designer to design team management

Key Decisions
01

Pivoting from crowdfunding to capital markets

Context

Crowdfunding was growing but commoditizing. Capital markets needed technology but no one was building for them.

Impact

This pivot led to the IPO. By moving into an underserved enterprise market, we found customers willing to pay enterprise prices and partner long-term. The crowdfunding platform's UX foundations transferred directly to investor onboarding flows.

Crowdfunding → Capital Markets Pivot
02

Building the design system before hiring designers

Context

Most companies hire designers first, then build systems. I built the system while I was still the only designer.

Impact

When new designers joined, they shipped production-quality work in days instead of weeks. The system encoded years of domain knowledge — regulatory constraints, accessibility patterns, multi-jurisdiction requirements — so new team members didn't have to learn it all from scratch.

Design System — Built Before Team

Reflection
✓ What Worked

1. Designer AND co-founder meant design had a seat at every decision — product, strategy, hiring, fundraising. Design wasn't an afterthought; it drove business outcomes.

2. Building the design system before hiring designers meant new team members were productive in days. The system encoded domain knowledge that would have taken months to learn.

3. Staying hands-on as Head of Design — I never fully stopped designing. This kept me connected to real user problems and gave the team a leader who understood the craft.

◈ What I'd Do Differently

1. Formalize user research earlier — in the early years, research was informal observation. Structured research would have caught usability issues sooner and built a stronger evidence base for design decisions.

2. Accessibility from day one — we retrofitted accessibility years after launch. Building it into the design system from the start would have saved significant rework.

3. Separate the founder hat from the designer hat sooner — wearing both meant I sometimes prioritized business speed over design quality. Recognizing when to switch modes earlier would have produced better outcomes.
