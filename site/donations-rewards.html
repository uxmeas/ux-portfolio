Crowdfunding Built for Local Arts Communities

InvestYYC. $500K+ raised. 2,000 donors. 45 arts projects funded across a 4-year run with Calgary Arts Development, ATB Financial, and Alberta Foundation for the Arts.

Role
Product Designer and Co-founder
Timeline
2012 to 2016 (InvestYYC platform: launched Nov 2012, closed Jan 1 2016)
Partners
Calgary Arts Development (administrator), ATB Financial (banking and matching partner), Alberta Foundation for the Arts
Platform origin
JOI Media (2008 founding), the white-label crowdfunding software that became the foundation for InvestYYC
$500K+
Total raised across 4 years (verified via Canada Media Fund writeup).
2,000 donors
Community funders supporting local arts.
45 arts projects
Dance, film, music, visual arts, and literary arts funded across the platform's full run.
( 01 ) The Problem

Calgary arts organizations had nowhere to fundraise locally with institutional backing. Artists used Kickstarter, which gave them none of what they actually needed. No tax receipts for Canadian donors. No institutional curation. No branding control. And no mechanism for arts councils or city cultural bodies to administer the process. Arts councils had zero visibility into campaign performance, fund distribution, or donor behavior. The tools existed. None of them were built for a Canadian community-arts context.

( 02 ) How It Was Framed

The platform was the first major client build on top of JOI Media's white-label crowdfunding software, launched to support Calgary's year as Canada's Cultural Capital. The team partnered with Calgary Arts Development (programmatic administration), ATB Financial (banking plus a $50K matching program), and Alberta Foundation for the Arts (institutional backing). The insight wasn't discovered in a research session. It was in the tax code: Canadian donors to registered charities could claim gifts as deductions, which meant a $500 donation to an arts project cost the donor substantially less than $500 in after-tax terms. Without automated tax receipts, that economic reality couldn't reach the platform.

HMW

How might we build a curated, Canadian-compliant crowdfunding platform that connects local artists with community donors, with donation matching, tax receipts, and full branding control for arts councils?

( 03 ) The Answer
A / 01

Curation built institutional trust

Calgary Arts Development reviewed every campaign before it launched. That curation layer was what ATB Financial needed to attach matching funds to the platform. It was also what donors needed to trust that their money was going to legitimate artistic work rather than opaque solo projects. The UX had to make curation feel supportive, not gatekeeping. Artists saw their application status, got feedback, and could resubmit. The whole workflow was branded as partnership with the arts council rather than judgment.

A / 02

Matching funds created launch urgency

ATB Financial committed $50K in matching funds across the platform's first run. The design decision was how to surface "match remaining" in real time across every campaign. A donor landing on a project page needed to know instantly: a $100 gift triggered another $100 from ATB's pool, up to the remaining match. The platform distributed the full $50K match in under 2 months. 30+ projects benefited in the first run.

A / 03

Electronic tax receipts made the Canadian advantage visible

Donors received a tax receipt immediately after completing their gift. Previously, Canadian donors using Kickstarter had to wait for the creator to independently send a paper receipt (which many creators didn't know how to do, and platforms didn't facilitate). On InvestYYC, the receipt arrived in the inbox before the confirmation page loaded. The platform handled CRA-compliant formatting, donation categorization, and year-end summary generation. The donor could file tax deductions without ever opening a PDF or calling the charity.

A / 04

White-label branding made the arts council the front door

InvestYYC didn't look like Katipult or JOI Media. It looked like Calgary Arts Development. The platform's branding, typography, color palette, domain, and voice were all the arts council's. The underlying software was invisible. That was the product decision that made the arts council a genuine partner rather than a client of a tech company. The same infrastructure later powered ATB Booster.

( 04 ) Key Screen Annotations
SCREEN 01

Campaign Page

Each campaign page showcased the artist, the project, the matching fund status, and the donor list. Curated content from the arts council framed the project's artistic context.

SCREEN 02

Matching Fund Widget

A live counter on every campaign page surfaced how much of ATB's $50K pool remained and how much a given gift amount would trigger in matched funds. Transparent math in real time, visible before the donor committed. This single widget is what drove the full $50K to distribute in under 2 months.

SCREEN 03

Donor Receipt Flow

Post-donation confirmation that doubled as a tax receipt. CRA-compliant formatting. Inline for immediate confirmation, emailed for record-keeping.

SCREEN 04

Arts Council Admin

Curation queue for new campaigns, matching fund distribution tracker, donor analytics across all campaigns, reporting exports for grant compliance.

( 05 ) Results

$500K+ raised · 2,000 donors · 45 arts projects funded · $50K matched in 2 months · 4-year platform run (Nov 2012 to Jan 2016)

( 06 ) Reflection
What worked

Institutional partnership (Calgary Arts Development plus ATB plus Alberta Foundation for the Arts) made the platform viable where solo crowdfunding tools weren't. Electronic tax receipts made the Canadian advantage visible and converted donors who would have walked away without one. Matching funds drove launch urgency and gave ATB a measurable return on their community investment. White-label branding made the arts council the front door, which is what made the partnership genuine. When Calgary flooded in 2013, the platform flexed to support flood recovery campaigns alongside the arts roster, which showed the infrastructure had community utility beyond its original scope.

What to do differently

Faster campaign approval cycles. The 1-month curation review, combined with a 3-month wait for artists to receive funds after campaign close and a registration window that sometimes ran up to a year before an artist could launch, made InvestYYC noticeably slower than Kickstarter and Indiegogo. Artists who'd already been through the cycle once started going back to Kickstarter for speed. A 2-week fast-track for returning artists would have retained community momentum. Earlier mobile-first: donation flows were retrofitted for mobile, and a significant portion of donor drop-off in 2014 and 2015 was mobile-specific. Honest lesson: the platform declined in its last two years (18 projects in 2014, 10 in 2015) partly because the approval bureaucracy outweighed the tax-receipt and matching advantage for smaller campaigns. Scale the governance down for smaller asks, and the model still holds.
