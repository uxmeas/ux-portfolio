( 01 ) The Problem

Every random selection tool produces a result instantly. That's the problem. The result isn't interesting. The anticipation is. When a teacher picks a student, a fantasy league picks a draft order, or a group picks a restaurant, everyone in the room has a stake in the outcome. Instant reveal removes the only part of the experience that creates engagement. Existing randomizers are also solitary by default. One person runs the tool and announces the result. There's no shared viewing experience, no reason for the group to watch together, and no moment that anyone will remember.

( 02 ) How It Was Framed

The insight came from observing group decision-making moments. The value of a random selection tool isn't randomness. Randomness is a solved math problem. The value is making the selection feel like an event. A 15-second race where everyone watches and reacts is 100 times more memorable than reading a result off a screen. Sound design observation: muting any competitive event (sports, games, races) cuts the emotional impact dramatically. Sound wasn't an add-on. It was half the product.

HMW

How might we make random selection feel like entertainment, with the suspense of a race, the engagement of shared participation, and the utility of a real decision tool?

( 03 ) The Answer
A / 01

Elimination race over instant reveal

Instead of computing a result and displaying it, MyPick.io runs a visual race where all participants move across the screen simultaneously. One finishes first. That's the winner. The math is still random. The race outcome is determined before it starts. But the experience is 15 to 120 seconds of visual tension. The race duration is configurable. A 15-second race for a quick classroom pick. A 120-second race for a fantasy draft where everyone has time to get invested. The dynamic camera follows the race leader, creating a broadcast-quality focal point that makes it feel like watching a real race rather than watching an animation.

A / 02

Multiplayer rooms eliminated the "one person reads the result" problem

6-character room codes let anyone join a race in progress with no account required and no app to download. The host controls the race. Viewers mirror the exact same state via Supabase Realtime with 100% sync accuracy. No viewer sees a different leader than the host at any moment. When 20 people in a room watch the same race on 20 different devices simultaneously, it becomes a shared experience. The result becomes something that happened to the group rather than something reported to the group.

A / 03

Sound as first-class design, not decoration

24 sound effects were integrated before the visual design was finalized. A deliberate choice to establish sound as a core experience component, not an afterthought. Crowd cheers when the race starts. Engine revs on acceleration. Collision impacts when teams jostle for position. Victory fanfares at the finish. Every sound was tested against the visual to confirm it enhanced rather than competed. The test for each sound: does removing it make the experience feel noticeably less complete? 24 effects passed that test.

( 04 ) Key Screen Annotations
SCREEN 01

Race View

Teams rendered as pixel vehicles racing across a scrolling track. Dynamic camera tracks the leader with a lag that creates cinematic anticipation. The camera catches up, revealing who's ahead. Billboards line the track. Weather effects change the atmosphere. Night mode available for lower-light environments.

SCREEN 02

Multiplayer Room Lobby

6-character code displayed prominently. QR code available for instant mobile join. Participant list updates in real time as viewers join. Host controls are separated from the viewer interface. Viewers see the race. Hosts see the controls.

( 05 ) Results

25 teams per race · 24 sound effects · 100% multiplayer sync · 6-character room codes · 15 to 120 second configurable duration · No accounts, no downloads for viewers

( 06 ) Reflection
What worked

Emotion-first design. Building for suspense and engagement rather than pure utility created something people describe as fun rather than useful, which is much harder to achieve and much stickier. The authoritative host pattern solved multiplayer sync problems completely on the first attempt.

What to do differently

Mobile-first layout for the race view. The track design was built for projectors and desktop screens, the most common use case for teachers and event hosts. Mobile viewers in multiplayer rooms got a suboptimal experience. Analytics tracking from day one would have revealed race duration preferences, team count distributions, and replay behavior that could have informed the next feature set.
