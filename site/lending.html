CS-04 · LENDING · FINTECH · PLATFORM EXTENSION

Private Lending for an Equity Platform. Without Building a Second Product.

Extended a private capital markets platform from equity-only to full debt support. Designed for ATB Financial's Crown-corp-bank small business lending workflow.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Timeline
2016 to 2020
Lead client
ATB Financial (Alberta Treasury Branches, Crown corporation bank)
International deployments
Minterest (Singapore SME lending), Oblix Capital (UK alternative lending), Initiative Ireland (Irish community and property finance), Avana Capital (US commercial real estate lending)
5 country deployments
Shipped lending in Canada (ATB Financial), Singapore (Minterest), UK (Oblix Capital), Ireland (Initiative Ireland), and US (Avana Capital) on one shared platform.
Zero second product
Extended an equity-only platform into full debt support without forking the codebase, without a second login, and without confusing existing equity users.
28-page specification
Anchored the architecture to ATB Financial's Crown-corporation-bank requirements for small business lending plus microfinance.
( 01 ) The Problem

Building a second standalone product for private lending would have cost 18 months, created an untenable support and maintenance split, and forced clients managing both equity and debt to maintain two separate logins and two different mental models. But the alternative wasn't simple. Debt UX is fundamentally different from equity UX. Equity users think in shares, ownership percentages, and exit events. Debt users think in repayment schedules, interest accrual, amortization, and default risk. Bolting lending onto an equity product without rethinking the information architecture would confuse both user bases and introduce compliance errors that could be commercially catastrophic.

( 02 ) How It Was Framed

The 28-page platform specification for ATB Financial anchored the requirements. Small business lending plus microfinance, delivered through a Crown corporation bank subject to Canadian banking regulations. The specification forced clarity on three user journeys that the equity platform didn't have: lender (browse, invest, manage portfolio), borrower (apply, fund, repay), administrator (approve, monitor, report). The team benchmarked against Juniper Square (US investment management), Introcrowd (UK land investment), and Minterest (Singapore SME lending) to map the edges of what a multi-market lending platform needed. The critical insight: users didn't need separate products. They needed the platform to know which asset class they were working in and adapt accordingly.

HMW

How might we extend an equity-focused platform to support private lending, without building a separate product or introducing confusion for existing equity users, while handling multiple loan types (amortized, interest-only, balloon) and TFSA/RRSP Canadian investment vehicles?

( 03 ) The Answer
A / 01

Contextual UI over separate products

Three architectural options were evaluated. A separate lending application, a tabbed interface switching between asset classes, and a contextual UI that adapted based on user role and asset class assignment. The separate app would have doubled maintenance. The tabbed interface would have exposed lending UI to equity users who had no need for it. Contextual adaptation was the correct answer. A shared infrastructure layer for users, documents, and compliance, with asset-class-specific interfaces surfaced on top. Equity users never saw lending complexity. Lending users never saw equity noise. Administrators with access to both worked from a single unified dashboard.

A / 02

Trulioo plus Onfido handled KYC across borrower types

ATB needed to verify identity for borrowers who might be a small business owner, a sole proprietor, an incorporated microbusiness, or an individual applying for personal debt. Each had different document requirements. Trulioo's global identity database provided the data layer. Onfido's document scan plus selfie biometric provided the document layer. The team designed the UX so borrowers experienced a single flow regardless of which backend path fired. Manual fallback existed for edge cases where automated confidence was too low.

A / 03

Auto-invest rules engine matched lender appetite to loan supply

Lenders set criteria (risk profile, time horizon, loan size, country, industry) and the system automatically allocated deposits to matching loans as they became available. The UI made the rule set visible as a plain-English filter stack. A lender could say "Canadian small business loans under $50K, 24-month term, manufacturing or services sectors only, risk grade B or higher" and see in real time how many current loans matched. Portfolio diversification handled itself while the lender slept.

A / 04

Default escalation as a primary workflow

In equity, a failed investment is a write-off. The UX path ends. In lending, a default triggers a complex regulatory and legal escalation. Formal notices, cure periods with defined timelines, potential legal action, collateral seizure procedures (for secured loans), and reporting to credit bureaus. Designing default handling as a first-class workflow (with structured state transitions, required documentation, notification templates, and multi-jurisdiction compliance mapping) meant fund managers could trust the system before the first loan originated. Not after the first default.

A / 05

TFSA and RRSP integration made the platform uniquely Canadian

Canadian investors expect lending returns to flow into tax-advantaged wrappers. The platform integrated TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account), RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan), and standard cash accounts. Each had different contribution limits, different tax treatment, and different annual reporting obligations. The team designed the account-selection UX so a lender picking their deposit account saw the relevant annual contribution room without needing to consult a separate CRA statement.

( 04 ) Key Screen Annotations
SCREEN 01

Loan Creation Wizard

Step-by-step configuration from term sheet through interest setup and borrower verification. Debt-specific fields (amortization schedule, interest rate type, balloon payment configuration) that have no equity equivalent. Equity users never encounter this wizard.

SCREEN 02

Repayment Schedule View

Visual timeline showing every payment across the loan term in a single scroll. Color-coded payment status (paid, upcoming, overdue, defaulted). Click any payment to open the detail row.

SCREEN 03

Auto-Invest Rule Builder

Plain-English filter stack where lenders defined allocation criteria. Real-time match count showing how many current loans met the criteria, and how much of the lender's capital would be deployed if they activated the rule.

SCREEN 04

Default Escalation Timeline

Structured view of notices sent, cure periods elapsed, and required next actions. Automated where possible, flagged for administrator action where not.

( 05 ) Results

Full lending lifecycle from creation through default · Trulioo and Onfido KYC integration · TFSA, RRSP, and cash account support · Multiple loan types (amortized, interest-only, balloon, single payment) · E-wallet payment processing (deposit, withdrawal, transfer) · Guest registration with social login plus progressive profile completion · Unified admin for both asset classes · Deployed across ATB Financial (Canada), Minterest (Singapore), Oblix Capital (UK), Initiative Ireland, Avana Capital (US)

( 06 ) Reflection
What worked

Contextual adaptation kept both user bases satisfied. Neither group encountered UI meant for a different asset class. Treating default escalation as a primary workflow earned immediate trust from lending clients who expected edge cases to be an afterthought. Global KYC vendor integration (Trulioo plus Onfido) scaled identity verification across 5 country deployments without requiring country-specific UX work.

What to do differently

Earlier user testing with actual borrowers (not just lenders). Most early wireframes used lender mental models as proxies for borrower mental models, and several of those assumptions turned out wrong when real small business owners hit the flow. Mobile-first for borrower applications. The first release was desktop-only, and borrowers increasingly expected mobile for payment confirmations and statement access. Earlier integration of credit bureau reporting APIs. The default escalation workflow was designed assuming manual bureau reporting, which worked but added weeks to each default cycle. Automation there would have scaled cleaner.
